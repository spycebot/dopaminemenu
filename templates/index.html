Claude: A dopamine menu is a personalized list of activities organized by how much time and energy they require, designed to help you find healthy ways to boost your mood and motivation throughout the day.

How to Create Your Dopamine Menu

  1. Divide activities into categories:
    • Appetizers - Quick 5-15 minute mood boosters you can do anytime (stretching, listening to a favorite song, stepping outside, petting your dog, doodling)
    • Entrees - Main activities that take 30 minutes to a few hours and provide deeper satisfaction (exercising, working on a hobby, calling a friend, cooking a nice meal, reading)
    • Sides - Things you can pair with less enjoyable tasks to make them more pleasant (listening to podcasts while cleaning, playing music while working, lighting a candle during admin tasks)
    • Desserts - High-dopamine activities you should enjoy in moderation since they can become unhealthy if overdone (social media scrolling, binge-watching shows, video games, online shopping)
  2. Make it personal: List activities you genuinely enjoy, not what you think you "should" enjoy. Your menu will look different from anyone else's.
  3. Keep it visible: Put your menu somewhere you'll see it when you're feeling unmotivated, bored, or tempted to default to less healthy dopamine sources.
  4. Use it strategically: When you need a boost, consult your menu based on how much time and energy you have available. This helps you make intentional choices rather than mindlessly reaching for your phone.
  5. The goal is building awareness of what genuinely helps you feel good and creating easy access to those options when you need them.

Gemini: A Dopamine Menu (or "Dopamenu") is a curated list of activities designed to give you a healthy hit of dopamine without the "hangover" that comes from endless scrolling or binge-watching.

The goal isn't to ban pleasure, but to ensure you have a prepared list of intentional sparks for when you're bored, under-stimulated, or stressed.

Implementation Tips

  • Write it down: Don't keep it in your head. Put it on a physical piece of paper, a fridge whiteboard, or a pinned note on your phone.
  • Remove friction: If "Painting" is a Main Course, keep your supplies accessible. If you have to dig through a closet to find them, you’ll just pick up your phone instead.
  • Know your "Ingredients": Pay attention to what actually makes you feel better versus what just makes you feel numb.

The Golden Rule: When you feel that "itch" to scroll, check your menu first. Pick a Starter or a Side before you're allowed to touch the Dessert.
